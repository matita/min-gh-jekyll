I mentioned recently, buried in a post about UI changes, that Twitter‚Äôs latest earnings report included this bombshell:

We are going to fix the broken windows and confusing parts, like the .@name syntax and @reply rules, that we know inhibit usage and drive people away

There‚Äôs an interesting problem here. UI is hard. You can‚Äôt just slap a button on the screen for every feature that could conceivably be used at any given time. Some features are only of interest to so-called ‚Äúpower users‚Äù, so they‚Äôre left subtle, spread by word-of-mouth. Some features you try to make invisible and heuristic. Some features are added just to solve one influential user‚Äôs problem. Some features are, ah, accidental.

A sufficiently mature, popular, and interesting product thus tends to accumulate a small pile of hidden features, sometimes not documented or even officially acknowledged. I‚Äôd say this is actually a good thing! Using something for a while should absolutely reward you with a new trick every so often ‚Äî that below-the-surface knowledge makes you feel involved with the thing you‚Äôre using and makes it feel deeper overall.

The hard part is striking a balance. On one end of the spectrum you have tools like Notepad, where the only easter egg is that pressing F5 inserts the current time. On the other end you have tools like vim, which consist exclusively of easter eggs.

One of Twitter‚Äôs problems is that it‚Äôs tilted a little too far towards the vim end of the scale. It looks like a dead-simple service, but those humble 140 characters have been crammed full of features over the years, and the ways they interact aren‚Äôt always obvious. There are rules, and the rules generally make sense once you know them, but it‚Äôs also really easy to overlook them.

Here, then, is a list of all the non-obvious things about Twitter that I know. Consider it both a reference for people who aren‚Äôt up to their eyeballs in Twitter, and an example of how these hidden features can pile up. I‚Äôm also throwing in a couple notes on etiquette, because I think that‚Äôs strongly informed by the shape of the platform.

Text

  • Tweets are limited to 140 Unicode characters, meaning that even astral plane characters (such as emoji) only count as one.

  • Leading and trailing whitespace is stripped from tweets.

  • Tweets may contain newlines, and there doesn‚Äôt seem to be any limit to how many.

  • In the middle of a tweet, strings of whitespace (e.g. multiple spaces) are preserved. However, more than two consecutive newlines will be reduced to only two.

  • Anything remotely resembling a link will be mangled into some http://t.co/asdf link-shortened garbage. In some cases, such as when talking about a domain name, this can make the tweet longer. You can defeat this by sticking an invisible character, such as U+200D ZERO WIDTH JOINER, around the final dot so it no longer looks like a domain name.

  • For the sake of its SMS-based roots, Twitter supports performing several commands by typing them in a tweet. In particular, if you start a tweet with the word d or dm, the second word will be treated as a username, and the rest of the tweet will be DM‚Äôd to that user.

  • Accounts managed by multiple people, such as support accounts or politicians‚Äô accounts, sometimes sign tweets with a ^ followed by the author‚Äôs initials. This has no special significance to Twitter.

  • You cannot use astral plane characters (which includes most emoji) in your display name or bio; they will be silently stripped. However, you can use anything from the Miscellaneous Symbols or Dingbats blocks, and many of these characters are rendered with color glyphs on Web Twitter. Results may vary on phones and in other clients.

Replies and mentions

A tweet can ‚Äúmention‚Äù other users, which just means including their @handle somewhere in the tweet. This will notify every mentioned user of the tweet.

  • A tweet that begins with a mention ‚Äî that is, the very first character is @ and it‚Äôs immediately followed by an extant username ‚Äî won‚Äôt appear on your profile on Web Twitter. It‚Äôll still appear on the ‚Äúwith replies‚Äù page. It‚Äôll also appear on your profile on Android Twitter, which doesn‚Äôt separate replies from not.

  • A tweet that begins with a mention won‚Äôt appear on the timelines of anyone who follows you, unless they also follow the first person you mention. That is, if you tweet @foo @bar heya, it‚Äôll only appear on the timelines of people who follow both you and @foo.

  • If you put some other character before the first @, the previous rule no longer applies, and your tweet will appear to all your followers. So .@foo @bar heya will be visible to everyone (and show on your Web profile). This is called ‚Äúdot-replying‚Äù. The dot isn‚Äôt actually special; it‚Äôs just an easy-to-type and unobtrusive character. I like to use ‚Üí or \. Some people prefer to put the mentions at the end instead, producing heya @foo @bar.

You can reply to tweets, which threads them together. A tweet can only have one parent (or no parent), but any number of replies. Everything on Twitter is thus arranged into a number of trees, where the root of the tree is a new tweet not responding to anything, and replies branch out from there.

  • A reply must, somewhere, mention the author of the tweet it‚Äôs replying to. If you reply to a tweet and delete the author‚Äôs @handle, it‚Äôll become a new top-level tweet rather than a reply.

  • There is one exception to the previous rule: if you‚Äôre replying to yourself, you don‚Äôt have to include your own @handle, even though clients include it by default. So if you want to say something that spans multiple tweets, you can just keep replying to yourself and deleting the @handle.

    It‚Äôs a really good idea to do this whenever you‚Äôre making multiple tweets about something. Otherwise, someone who stumbles upon one of the tweets later will have no idea what the context was, and won‚Äôt be able to find it without scrolling back however long on your profile.

    If you reply to yourself but leave your @handle at the beginning, the first tweet will appear on your profile, but the others won‚Äôt, because they start with a mention.

    I‚Äôm told that many third-party clients don‚Äôt support replying to yourself without your handle included, and the API documentation doesn‚Äôt mention that it‚Äôs a feature. But I‚Äôm also told that only first-party clients require you to mention someone in a reply in order to thread, and that third-party clients will merrily thread anything to anything. (I remember when Web Twitter allowed that, so I totally believe the API still does.) If you don‚Äôt use the official clients, I guess give it a whirl and see what happens.

  • The previous rule also applies when making longer replies to someone else. Reply to them once, then reply to yourself with the next tweet (and remove your own @handle). You‚Äôll end up with three tweets all threaded together.

    This is even more important, because Twitter shows the replies to a tweet in a somewhat arbitrary order, bubbling ‚Äúimportant‚Äù ones to the top. If you write a very long response and break it across three tweets, all replying to the same original tweet, they‚Äôll probably show as an incoherent jumble to anyone reading the thread. If you make each tweet a reply to the previous one, they‚Äôre guaranteed to stay in order.

  • Replying to a tweet will also prefill the @handle of anyone mentioned in the tweet. Replying to a retweet will additionally prefill the @handle of the person who retweeted it. In some cases, it may be polite to remove some of these; you only need the original author‚Äôs @handle to make a reply. (It‚Äôs not uncommon to accumulate multiple mentions, then end up in an extended conversation with only one other person, while constantly notifying several third parties.)

  • In official clients (Web and Android, at least), long threads of tweets are collapsed on your timeline. Only the first tweet and the last two tweets are visible. If you have a lot to say about something, it‚Äôs a good idea to put the important bits in one of those three tweets where your followers will actually see them. This is another reason it‚Äôs polite to thread your tweets together ‚Äî it saves people from having their timelines flooded by your tweetstorm.

    Sometimes, it‚Äôs possible to see multiple ‚Äúbranches‚Äù of the same conversation on your timeline. For example, if A makes a few tweets, and B and C both reply, and you follow all three of them, then you‚Äôll see B‚Äôs replies and C‚Äôs replies separately. Clients don‚Äôt handle this particularly well and it can become a bit of a clusterfuck, with the same root tweet appearing multiple times.

  • Because official clients treat a thread as a single unit, you can effectively ‚Äúbump‚Äù your own tweet by replying to it. Your reply is new, so it‚Äôll appear on your followers‚Äô timelines; but the client will also include the first tweet in the thread as context, regardless of its age.

  • When viewing a single tweet, official clients may not show the replies in chronological order. Usually the ‚Äúbest‚Äù replies are bumped to the top. ‚ÄúBest‚Äù is entirely determined by Twitter, but it seems to work fairly well. If you reply to yourself, your own replies will almost certainly appear first.

  • If reply to a tweet with @foo heya, and @foo later renames their account to @quux, the tweet will retain its threading even though it no longer mentions the author of the parent tweet. However, your reply will now appear on your profile, because it doesn‚Äôt begin with the handle of an existing user. Note that this means it‚Äôs fairly easy for a non-follower to figure out what you renamed your account to, by searching for replies to your old name.

  • Threads are preserved even if some of the tweets are hidden (either because you‚Äôve blocked some participants, or because they have their accounts set to private). Those tweets won‚Äôt appear for you, but any visible replies to them will.

  • If a tweet in the middle of a thread is deleted (or the author‚Äôs account is deleted), the thread will break at that point. Replies to the deleted tweet won‚Äôt be visible when looking at the parent, and the parent won‚Äôt be visible when looking at the replies.

  • You can quote tweets by including a link to them in your tweet, which will cause the quoted tweet to appear in a small box below yours. This does not create a reply and will not be part of the quoted tweet‚Äôs thread. If you want to do that, you can‚Äôt use the retweet/quote button. You have to reply to the tweet, manually include a link to it, and be sure to mention the author.

  • When you quote a tweet, the author is notified; however, unlike a retweet, they won‚Äôt be notified when people like or retweet your quote (unless you also mention them). If you don‚Äôt want to notify the author, you can take a screenshot (though this doesn‚Äôt let them delete the tweet) or use a URL shortener (though this doesn‚Äôt let them obviously disable a quote by blocking you).

  • Due to the nature of Twitter, it‚Äôs common for a tweet to end up on many people‚Äôs timelines simultaneously and attract many similar replies within a short span of time. It‚Äôs polite to check the existing replies to a popular tweet, or a tweet from a popular person, before giving your two cents.

  • It‚Äôs generally considered rude to barge into the middle of a conversation between two other people, especially if they seem to know each other much better than you know them, and especially if you‚Äôre being antagonistic. There are myriad cases where this may be more or less appropriate, and no hard and fast rules. You‚Äôre a passerby overhearing two people talking on the street; act accordingly.

  • When you quote or publicly mention someone for the sake of criticizing them, be aware that you‚Äôre exposing them to all of your followers, some of whom may be eager for an argument. If you have a lot of followers, you might inadvertently invite a dogpile.

Hashtags

Hashtags are a # character followed by some number of non-whitespace characters.

  • Exactly which characters may appear in a hashtag is somewhat inconsistent, and has quietly changed at least once.

  • The only real point to hashtags is that you can click on them in clients to jump directly to search results.

  • Hashtags can appear in the ‚Äútrending‚Äù widget, but so can any other regular text.

  • There is no reason to tag a bunch of random words in your tweets. No one is searching Twitter for #funny. Doing this makes you look like an extremely out-of-touch marketer.

  • Twitter also supports ‚Äúcashtags‚Äù, which are prefixed with a $ instead and are generally stock symbols. I only even know this because it makes shell and Perl code look goofy.

Media

A tweet may have one embedded attachment.

  • You may explicitly include a set of up to four images or a video or a poll. You cannot combine this within a single tweet. Brands‚Ñ¢ have access to a handful of other embedded gizmos.

  • If you include images or a video, you will lose 24 characters of writing space, because a direct link to the images/video will be silently added to the end of your tweet. This is for the sake of text-only clients, e.g. people using Twitter over SMS, so they can see that there‚Äôs an attachment and possibly view it in a browser.

  • Including a poll will not append a link, but curiously, you‚Äôll still lose 24 characters. It‚Äôs possible this is a client bug, but it happens in both Web and Android Twitter.

  • Alternative clients may not support new media types at first. In particular, people who used TweetDeck were frequently confused right after polls were launched, because TweetDeck showed only the tweet text and no indication that a poll had ever been there. Some third-party clients still don‚Äôt support polls. Consider mentioning when you‚Äôre using a new attachment type. Might I suggest prefixing your tweet with üìä?

  • If you don‚Äôt include an explicit attachment, Twitter will examine the links in your tweet, in reverse order. If you link to a tweet, that tweet will be quoted in yours. If you link to a website that supports Twitter ‚Äúcards‚Äù (small brief descriptions of a site, possibly with images), that card will be attached. There can only be one attachment, so as soon as Twitter finds something it can use, it stops looking.

  • You can embed someone else‚Äòs media in your own tweet by ending it with a link to the media URL ‚Äî that is, the one that ends with /photo/1. This is different from a quoted tweet, and won‚Äôt notify the original tweeter.

  • Quoted tweets are always just tweets that include links to other tweets. Even if the tweet is deleted, an embed box will still appear, though it‚Äôll be grayed out and say the tweet is unavailable.

    If the link is the last thing to appear in the tweet text, official clients will not show the link. This can be extremely confusing if you try to link to two tweets ‚Äî the first one will be left as a regular link, and the second one will be replaced by a quoted tweet, so at a glance it looks like you linked to a tweet and it was also embedded. A workaround for this is just to add text after the final link, so it‚Äôs not the last thing in the tweet and thus isn‚Äôt hidden.

  • Twitter cards may be associated with a Twitter account. On Android Twitter (not Web Twitter!), replying to a tweet with a card will also include the @handle for the associated account. For example, replying to a tweet that links to a YouTube video will prefill @YouTube. This is pretty goofy, since YouTube itself didn‚Äôt make the video, and it causes replies to notify the person even though the original link doesn‚Äôt.

  • Uploaded media may be flagged as ‚Äúsensitive‚Äù, which generally means ‚Äúpornographic‚Äù. This will require viewers to click through a warning to see the media, unless they‚Äôre logged in and have their account set to skip the warning. Flagged media also won‚Äôt appear in the sidebar on profile pages for people who have the warning enabled.

  • The API supports marking individual tweets as containing sensitive media, but official clients do not ‚Äî instead, there‚Äôs an account setting that applies to everything you upload from that point forward. Media may also be flagged by other users as sensitive. Twitter also has some sort of auto-detection for sensitive media, which I only know about because it sometimes thinks photos of my hairless cats are pornographic.

  • If your own tweets have ‚Äúsensitive‚Äù media attached, you will have to click through the warning, even if you have the warning disabled. A Twitter employee tells me this is so you‚Äôre aware when your own tweets are flagged, but the message still tells you to disable the warning in account settings, so this is mostly just confusing.

Blocking and muting

  • A blocked user cannot view your profile. They can, of course, use a different account, or merely log out. This is entirely client-side, too, so it‚Äôs possible that some clients don‚Äôt even support this ‚Äúfeature‚Äù.

  • A blocked user cannot like or retweet your tweets.

  • A blocked user cannot follow you. If you block someone who‚Äôs already following you, they‚Äôll be forced to immediately unfollow. Likewise, you cannot follow a blocked user.

  • A blocked user‚Äôs tweets won‚Äôt appear on your timeline, or in any thread. As of fairly recently, their tweets won‚Äôt appear in search results, either. However, if you view the profile of someone who‚Äôs retweeted a blocked user, you will still see that retweet.

  • A blocked user can see your tweets, if someone they follow retweets you.

  • A blocked user can mention or reply to you, though you won‚Äôt be notified either by the tweet itself or by any retweets/likes. However, if someone else replies to them, your @handle will be prefilled, and you‚Äôll be notified. Also, other people viewing your tweets will still see their replies threaded.

  • A blocked user can link to your tweets ‚Äî however, rather than an embedded quote, their tweet will have a gray ‚Äúthis tweet is unavailable‚Äù box attached. This effect is retroactive. However (I think?), if a quoted tweet can‚Äôt be shown, the link to the tweet is left visible, so people can still click it to view the tweet manually.

  • Muting has two different effects. If you mute someone you‚Äôre following, their tweets won‚Äôt appear in your timeline, but you‚Äôll still get notifications from them. This can be useful if you set your phone to only buzz on notifications from people you follow. If you mute someone you‚Äôre not following, nothing they do will send you notifications. Either way, their tweets will still be visible in threads and search results.

  • Relatedly, if you follow someone who‚Äôs a little eager with the retweeting, you can turn off just their retweets. It‚Äôs in the menu on their profile.

  • It‚Äôs trivial to tell whether someone‚Äôs blocked you, since their profile will tell you. However, it‚Äôs impossible to know for sure if someone has muted you or is just manually ignoring you, since being muted doesn‚Äôt actually prevent you from doing anything.

  • You can block and mute someone at the same time, though this has no special effect. If you unblock them, they‚Äôll just still be muted.

  • The API strips out tweets from blocked and muted users server-side for streaming requests (such as your timeline), but leaves it up to the client for other requests (such as viewing a single tweet). So it‚Äôs possible that a client will neglect to apply the usual rule of ‚Äúyou never see a blocked user‚Äôs tweets in threads‚Äù. In particular, I‚Äôve heard several reports that this is the case in the official iOS Twitter (!).

  • Tweeting screenshots of ‚Äúyou have been blocked‚Äù is getting pretty old and we can probably stop doing it.

Search

  • Almost all of Twitter‚Äôs advanced search options are exposed on the advanced search page. All of them are shorthand for using a prefix in your search query; for example, ‚Äúfrom these accounts‚Äù just becomes something like from:username.

  • The one that isn‚Äôt listed there is filter:, which is only mentioned in the API documentation. It can appear as filter:safe, filter:media, filter:images, or filter:links. It‚Äôs possible there are other undocumented forms.

  • Search applies to unshortened links, so you can find links to a website just by searching for its URL. However, because Twitter displays links without a protocol (http://), you have to leave it off when searching. Be aware that people who mention your work without mentioning you might be saying unkind things about it.

  • As a side effect, you can search for quotes of a given user‚Äôs tweets by searching for twitter.com/username/status, because all tweet URLs begin with that prefix. This will also include any tweets from that user that have photos or video attached, because attaching media appends a photo URL, but you can fix that by adding -from:username.

  • Searching for to:foo will only find tweets that begin with @foo; dot-replies and other mentions are not included. Searching for @foo will find mentions as well as tweets from that person. To find only someone‚Äôs mentions, you can search for @foo -from:foo. You can combine this with the above trick to find quotes as well.

  • I‚Äôve been told that from: only applies to the handle a user had when the tweet was made, but this doesn‚Äôt match my own experience. It‚Äôs possible the behavior is different depending on whether the old handle has been reclaimed by someone else.

  • Some clients, such as TweetDeck, support showing live feeds of search results right alongside your timeline and notifications. It‚Äôs therefore possible for people to keep an eye on a live stream of everyone who‚Äôs talking about them, even when their @handle isn‚Äôt mentioned. Bear this in mind when grumbling, especially about people whose attention you‚Äôd prefer to avoid.

  • Namesearch ‚Äî that is, look for mentions of you or your work that don‚Äôt actually @-mention you ‚Äî with caution. Liking or replying amicably to tweets that compliment you is probably okay. Starting arguments with people who dislike your work is rude and kind of creepy, and certainly not likely to improve anyone‚Äôs impression of you.

Locked accounts

  • You may set your account to private, which will hide your tweets from the general public. Only people who follow you will be able to see your tweets. Twitter calls this ‚Äúprotected‚Äù, but since it shows a lock icon next to your handle, everyone calls it ‚Äúlocked‚Äù.

  • Specifically: your banner, avatar, display name, and bio (including location, website, etc.) are still public. The number of tweets, follows, followers, likes, and lists you have are also public. Your actual tweets, media, follows, followers, lists, etc. are all hidden.

  • iOS Twitter hides the bio and numbers, as well, which is sort of inconvenient if you were using it to explain who you are and who you‚Äôre cool with having follow you.

  • When you lock your account, any existing followers will remain. Anyone else will only be able to send a follow request, which you can then approve or deny. You can force anyone to unfollow you at any time (whether locked or not) by blocking and then unblocking them. Or just blocking them.

  • Follow requests are easy to miss; only a few places in the UI make a point of telling you when you have new ones.

  • Approving or denying a follow request doesn‚Äôt directly notify the requester. If you approve, obviously they‚Äôll start seeing your tweets in their timeline. If you deny, the only difference is that if they look at your profile again, the follow button will no longer say ‚Äúpending‚Äù.

  • If you unlock your account, any pending follow requests are automatically accepted.

  • The only way to see your pending follows (accounts you have tried to follow that haven‚Äôt yet accepted) is via the API, or a client that makes use of the API. The official clients don‚Äôt show them anywhere.

  • No one can retweet a locked account, not even followers.

  • Quoting doesn‚Äôt work with locked account; the quoted tweet will only show the ‚Äúunavailable‚Äù message, even if a locked account quotes itself. Clicking the tweet link will still work, of course.

  • Locked accounts never create notifications for people who aren‚Äôt following them. A locked account can like, retweet, quote, follow, etc. as usual, and the various numbers will go up, but only their followers will be notified.

  • If a locked account likes a bunch of your tweets (or retweets, etc.), and then you follow them, the notifications will not be created retroactively.

  • Locked accounts do not appear in the lists of who liked or retweeted a tweet (except, of course, when viewed by someone following them).

  • While a locked account‚Äôs own follows and followers are hidden, a locked account will still appear publicly in the following/follower lists of other unlocked accounts. There is no blessed way to automatically cross-reference this, but be aware that the existence of a locked account is still public. In particular, if you follow someone who keeps an eye on their follower count, they can just look at their own list of followers to find you.

  • Anyone can still mention a locked account, whether or not they follow it, and it‚Äôll receive notifications.

  • Open DMs (‚Äúreceive direct messages from anyone‚Äù) work as normal for locked accounts. A locked account can send DMs to anyone with open DMs, and a locked account may turn on open DMs to receive DMs from anyone.

  • Replies to a locked account are not protected in any way. If a locked account participates in a thread, its own tweets will be hidden from non-followers, but any public tweets will be left in. Also, anyone can search for mentions of a locked account to find conversations it‚Äôs participated in, and may be able to infer what the locked account was saying from context.

API, other clients, etc.

I‚Äôve mentioned issues with non-primary clients throughout, but a couple more things to be aware of:

  • Web Twitter has some keyboard shortcuts, which you can view by pressing ?.

  • When I say Web Twitter throughout this document, I mean desktop Web Twitter; there‚Äôs also a mobile Web Twitter, which is much simpler.

  • The official API doesn‚Äôt support a number of Twitter features, including polls, ads, and DMs with multiple participants. Clients that use the API (i.e. clients not made by Twitter) thus cannot support these features.

  • Even TweetDeck, which is maintained by Twitter, frequently lags behind in feature support. TweetDeck had the original (client-side-only) implementation of muting, but even after Twitter added it as a real feature, TweetDeck was never changed to make use of it. So TweetDeck‚Äôs muting is separate from Twitter‚Äôs muting.

  • Tweets know what client they were sent from. Official Twitter apps don‚Äôt show this any more, but it‚Äôs still available in the API, and some alternative clients show it.

  • By default, Twitter allows people to find your account by searching for your email address or phone number. You may wish to turn this off.

  • Twitter has a ‚Äúcollections‚Äù feature, which lets you put any public tweets you like (even other people‚Äôs) in a group for other people to look over. However, no official client lets you create one; you have to do it via the API or TweetDeck. Collections aren‚Äôt listed anywhere (you have to link to them directly), and you can‚Äôt control the order of the tweets (they‚Äôre always reverse chronological).

  • Lists are a thing. I‚Äôve never really used them. They don‚Äôt support a lot of the features the regular timeline does; most notably, threaded tweets aren‚Äôt shown together. You can create a private list and add people to it to follow them without their knowledge, though.

  • You can ‚Äúpromote‚Äù a tweet, i.e. turn it into an ad, which is generally only of interest to advertisers. However, promoted tweets have the curious property that they don‚Äôt appear on your profile or in search results for anyone. It‚Äôs possible to target a promoted tweet at a specific list of users (or no one!), which allows for a couple creative hacks that you‚Äôll have to imagine yourself.

  • And then there‚Äôs the verified checkmark (given out arbitrarily), the power tools given to verified users (mysterious), the limits on duplicate tweets and follows and whatnot (pretty high), the analytics tools (pretty but pointless), the secret API-only notifications (Twitter tells you when your tweet is unfavorited!), the Web Twitter metadata that let me write a hack to hide mentions from non-followers‚Ä¶ you get the idea.
